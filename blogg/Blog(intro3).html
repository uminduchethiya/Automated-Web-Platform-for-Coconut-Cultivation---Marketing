CHAPTER 03

Biology of the Coconut Palm


Importance of Knowing Biology of Coconut

  • It helps to understand behavior of coconut in response to;

    • Different soil types

    • Change in weather conditions

    • Change in weather conditions

  • Family: Palmae

  • An important member of monocotyledons

  • Stem (Trunk):

    • Strong

    • Straight

    • Unbranched

    • Forms in about 4-5 years

    • Reaches 20 – 30 m in height at maturity

    • Carries a crown at the top


Crown of the coconut tree







Crown

  • Includes leaves (fronds) and a terminal bud (cabbage)
  • Crown bears 30 – 35 leaves
  • A coconut leaf grows up to 4 m in length
  • Unopened youngest leaf has been named as “Spear leaf” (Spindle leaf)
  • Youngest fully opened leaf has been named as “Flag leaf” (leaf no. 1)
  • Leaves arranged in 5 whorls, showing a spiral pattern
  • Alternate leaf arrangement intercept maximum amount of light
  • 12 – 14 fronds produced annually by a healthy palm

Inflorescence

  • Coconut palm is monoecious (It carries maleand female flowers on same Inflorescence)

  • Inflorescence is 60 – 150 cm long


  • Enclosed in outer and inner bracts

  • Born in leaf axil



  • Inner bract is deeply ribbed and grooved

  • Number of inflorescences produced per year are 12 – 14
    (corresponding to number of leaves)
    • (allows it to expand in girth)

  • Number of inflorescences produced per year are 12 – 14 (corresponding to number of leaves)

  • Open inflorescence consists of about 30 –40 spikelet (arising from peduncle)

  • Male flowers born on top half of the spikelet (and more numerous than female flowers)

  • Female flowers found at base of spikelet

  • Male phase completes in 20 days

  • Female phase lasts:
    • 3 – 5 days in tall varieties
    • 8 – 15 days in dwarf varieties

  • Normal inflorescence has:
    • 20 – 40 female flowers
    • 200 – 300 male flowers (about 50 –70% abort and drop off)




Pollination

  • Natural cross pollination by:

    • Insects (mainly honey bees)
    • Wind

  • Coconut palm is a continuous bearer: In a given time each palm has about 12 bunches with nuts (at different stages of development)

  • Large proportion of assimilates partitioned into developing nuts from the 4th bunch onwards

  • Left – handed coconut palms: coconut bunches hanging from left side of leaf supporting it

  • Right – handed coconut palms: coconut bunches fall on right side of leaf




Development Cycle of a Coconut Bunch

(Non- visual and visual cycles)

  • Inflorescence primodia begins to form in leaf axil

  • From: beginning of developing inflorescence, to:opening of inner bracts, it takes a time period of about 32 months (about 2 ½ years)

  • That time period is called as non-visual cycle

  • Non-visual cycle is followed by 12 months period to complete development of mature bunch (visual cycle) (can be seen by naked eye)


Immature nut fall:

  • First 3 – 4 months of developing bunch is sensitive to moisture stress

  • In 1st and 2nd months: 24% of immature nut fall

  • 3rd and 4th months: 40% of immature nut fall
    (most sensitive period for immature nut fall)

  • Tapping: artificial stimulation of unopened inflorescence to produce coconut sap (sweet toddy)

  • Coconut sap contains 14 – 18% sugars (mainly sucrosre)

  • Time for tapping: swelling at base of inflorescence (good indication)

  • Tapping improves yield of poorly bearing palms (once stopped, effect lasts for about 4 years resulting higher numberof female flowers)



Fruit (nut)

  • Botanically, fruit is a fibrous droop (popularly known termed "nut")

  • It takes, 11 – 12 months for female flowers to develop into mature bunch (after fertilization)

  • After 160 days, attains full size (and kernel begins to develop as a thin, jelly-like layer)

  • “Kurumba”: Tender nuts at about 220 days (7 months): water is, ideal for drinking since;
    • max. sugar content (4.5 ml/100 ml)

    • and, max. volume of water (300 ml/nut) occur at this stage)

  • Ripe nut is:
    • Ovoid
    • 3 angled
    • Has a single seed (embryo)



  • Embryo remains dormant for 6 weeks after picking

  • Coconut Shell (endocarp) marked by 3 depressions (eyes)

  • One is functional and soft (soft eye)

  • Embryo lies just below soft eye





Parts of a Matured Coconut



Kernel

  • Most valuable part of the palm

  • Consists with:
    • White meat (about ½” thick)
    • Thin brown testa

  • Kernel yield is inversely proportional to nut size

  • Wet kernel comprises 48% of the weight of a de-husked nut of 550 g

  • When stored for 3 – 4 weeks, wet kernel weight increased to 50 – 52 %

Root System

  • Accounts for 15% of total weight of palm

  • Arise from enlarged basal part of the stem (known as "bole")

  • No tap root. No root hairs

  • Numerous fibrous roots radiates from the 'bole'

  • Knowing the distribution of root system is essential to understand:

    • Effects of fertilizer use

    • Moisture conservation

    • Other cultural practices and …

    • Competition with crops and pastures

  • Greater concentration of roots (about 82%) in top 1 m soil depth with in a radius of 2 m from the 'bole'

  • Absorption region: lies 1 mm behind root cap.

  • Suberisation of root cells: when soils dry up, cells in absorbing region develop thickened walls (suberisation) and cease to absorb water and nutrients

  • That results set back in growth of young palm

  • Pneumatophores: on the surface of main roots and rootlets, there are respiratory roots called as pneumatophores

  • 75% of pneumatophores have distributed with in 1.2 m soil depth

  • Pneumatophores contribute to withstand water logging for a short period

  • Water logging for a long period forms thick root mat (bissa)

  • Coconut roots tolerate salinity levels up to concentration of salt in sea water (5% of NaCl)

  • So, coconut is widely distributed along coastal area
