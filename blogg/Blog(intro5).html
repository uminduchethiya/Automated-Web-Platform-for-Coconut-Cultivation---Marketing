CHAPTER 05

Climate and Soil for Coconut Cultivation


Climate and Soil

  • Coconut is well adapted to a range of soil types and climatic regions

  • Growth and yield of coconut is largely influenced by weather pattern and soil characteristics

Climate

  • Rainfall
    • The most important climatic factor (because it is grown as a rain fed crop)

    • Rain fall distribution over the year is more important than amount/year (because of a continuous bearer)

    • Coconut is cultivated in all three agro-climatic zones

    • Wet zone 30%
    Intermediate zone 50%
    Dry zone 20%

  • Optimum RF (Rainfall) regime is between 2500 mm – 1500 mm/yr

  • Wet zone (3500 mm/yr.) sets upper limit for coconut cultivation

  • Dry zone (1250 mm/yr.) sets lower limit for coconut cultivation


Rainfall and Nut Fall

  • Two dry periods occur

    • Dry periods(< 50 mm/month) Periods of immature & mature nut fall
    Jan/Feb Feb/March
    July/August August/September

    • Highest immature nut fall occurs immediately after rains following a dry period

    • Highest mature nut fall follows by a dry period with longer number of days (July/August)

    • Fluctuation of national coconut production is mainly influenced by intensity and distribution of RF



  • Current year’s crop is mainly determined by RF during previous year and first quarter of the current year

  • With in a year crop shows a considerable seasonal variation mainly due to RF

  • Coconut gives 6 picks/year

  • 6 picks/yr., ranking in descending order:
    P3 > P4 > P2 > P5 > P1 > P6

  • Picks, 2 + 3 + 4 gives 65% of the total crop

  • RF affects:
    • number and size of nuts

    • kernel thickness


Temperature

  • Ideal mean maximum temperature is around 290C (270 – 320C)

  • Average diurnal variation between 5 – 70C

  • Coconut does not thrive well with temperature <200C , which sets the limit of elevation (altitude) 500 – 600 m above sea level (upper limit for commercial coconut production)

Light

  • Coconut belongs to C3 plant (in terms of light use efficiency) (LUE)

  • LUE of an adult coconut palm is 1.4 g MJ-1

  • Minimum level of sunshine hours; 2000hrs/yr.(120 hrs./month)

  • Considerable variation in light interception by canopies of different ages

  • Average value of light intercepted by a palm is about 45%

  • In Wet zone, low sunshine hours due to cloud cover limits optimum production

  • In Intermediate zone, best combination of rainfall and sunshine hours facilitatesoptimum production

  • In Dry zone, low rainfall and uneven distribution limit optimum production

Soil

  • Soil:
    • physical (texture/structure, drainage, depth)

    • chemical (pH, CEC, C/N ratio)

    • and biological (fauna/flora) properties have a direct effect on growth and yield of palm

  • In the soil profile, there are 3 horizons:

    • A-horizon (0 -15 cm depth)

    • AB-horizon (15 -50 cm depth) and

    • B-horizon (50 -100 cm depth)

  • B-horizon is most important for coconut

  • Cultural practices should be aimed at improving B-horizon


Predominant soil types in coconut growing areas

  • Wet zone – lateritic soil

  • Intermediate zone – lateritic/gravelly soil, deep loamy coastal soil (latosols), deep sandy loam, alluvial/colluvial soil

  • Dry zone – Regosols (deep loamy coastal sand), latosols, alluvial soil


Optimum soil conditions for coconut

  • Deep sandy loam/loamy soils with good drainage and water holding capacity

  • Presence of water table within 2 – 3 m

  • Absence of hard pan within 2m depth

  • Soil pH: 5.5 – 7.5

    • High soil pH (pH>8) induces mineral deficiencies

    • Low soil pH (pH<4) releases micro-nutrients (Fe, Mn, Al) in toxic levels


Land suitability classes

  • Based on

    1. soil physical properties (soil depth, drainage, texture)

    2. and potential productivity of coconut; 5 land suitability classes have been identified in the coconut triangle

      • Highly suitable (S1and S2)

      • Suitable (S3)

      • Moderately suitable (S4)

      • Marginally suitable (S5)

  • This classification is a useful tool for making:

    1. location – specific recommendations of cultural practices and fertilizer use

    2. and provide a basis for intensification of land-use (intercropping/inter grazing)

  • S1and S2 – support highly yielding palms from well managed monoculture plantations
