Introduction


Importance of Coconut

Coconut (Cocos nucifera L), is one of the most important palm species of the humid tropics. Exact center of origin is unknown (because wild types spread widely). Possibly from South America before domesticating in Asia –Pacific regions. Coconut is a multipurpose tree species (MPTS).Coconut has earned the name “Tree of Life”,because it provides diverse uses to sustain (1) the coconut industry and (2)socio-cultural, religious activities. Coconut is grown in about 86 countries on a total land area of 12 million ha, producing about 53 billion nuts per year. About 90% concentrated in Asia and Pacific region. Sri Lanka is one of the largest coconut producing nation in the world, with approx. 420, 00 ha. Coconut palm is known to exist in Sri Lanka for over 2000 years. Coconut cultivation is dominated by smallholder’s sector (<8 ha) and accounts for 75% of the area, contributing 60% to the national coconut production. Coconut, provides 22% of calorie intake/person/day (as a source of dietary fat and proteins) Of total annual coconut production, 70% is used for domestic consumption.
Balance 30% used as raw material for processing industry, mainly for exports. Per capita consumption of coconut 120/year (90 fresh nuts + 30 nut equivalent of oil). Coconut sector provides livelihood for about 500,000 people, both as direct and indirect employment. National coconut production remains stagnant at around 2500 mil. nuts per year (500,000 mil. copra equivalent). Average coconut production: estimated at 7000 nuts/ha/yr. (45 nuts/palm/yr.). Economic life span of the palm is about 60 – 70 years and palms producing < 20 nuts/year due to senility should be replaced.

Factors contribute to low productivity

Poor adoption of
(1)cultivation and (2)management practices

Adverse weather conditions

Senile plantations

Land fragmentation

  • Due to (1) urbanization and (2) industrialization, about 400 ha of productive coconut lands are lost every year

  • By the application of new cultivation and management technologies developed by the CRI about 40% increase in nut yield (10,000 nutsha/yr. or 60 nuts/palm/yr.) achieved from existing plantations

  • Coconut is distributed in all 3 agro-climatic zones as follows:
    Wet zone – 29%
    Intermediate zone – 51%
    Dry zone – 20%

Coconut Industry

  • The coconut industry plays an important role in sustaining the national economy and food security of people

  • It comprises two main sectors engaged in
    • Coconut production sector
    • Coconut processing sector

  • Coconut processing sector comprises two main sub-sectors
    • Manufacture of coconut products and by products for the export market
    • Production and processing of coconut sap for domestic market


Institutional Framework

  • Coconut industry comes under the purview of the Ministry of Plantation Industries, dealing with policy matters relating to the overall development of the coconut industry

  • Government of Sri Lanka (GOSL) is supporting the coconut industry by providing financial assistance from (1) consolidated funds and (2) cess funds

  • There is a steady increase in the total allocation of funds for the coconut industry, but it represents a meager 3.5% of the total export earnings

  • The Ministry implements its policies thorough the following Statutory Boards:
    • Coconut Research Institute (CRI)
    • Coconut Cultivation Board (CCB)
    • Coconut Development Authority (CDA)


Current Problems in the Production Sector

  • Stagnation of coconut production is a serious threat to the sustainability and economic viability of the coconut industry

  • Reasons for stagnation:
    • Loss of coconut lands, particularly in the coconut triangle due to fragmentation of land.
    • Adverse weather conditions.
    • The decline in soil fertility.
    • Annual target for replanting and under-planting not achieved.
    • Instability of coconut prices and increasing cost of production.
    • Mismanagement of some private and state owned lands.
    • Shortage of skilled and unskilled labor.
    • High wage rates.


Problems of the Processing Sector

  • Lack of market information systems (MIS) to study marketing strategy in the international markets

  • Lack of stringent quality standards for kernel and non-kernel products to enhance global competiveness

  • Use of outdated machinery and traditional methods

  • Shortage of skilled workers for various operations(eg. Toddy tapping, husking, hatcheting)

  • Area covered by tapping has not expanded creating a shortage of toddy supply


Future Needs

  • Break the yield barrier toachieve sustainability and economic viability(Yield target in 2020: 4000 million nuts).

  • Stabilize and maintain the price for fresh coconuts(Reasonable price for both consumer and producer).

  • Ensure supply and demand of processing industry.

  • Strengthen research and extension linkages to promote effective technology transfer.

  • Meet quality standards set by international market to deal better with global competitiveness and increase our share of DC, white fiber and activated carbon.

  • Explore the possibility of introducing and marketing of new value added products (derived from kernel and non-kernel components).

  • Upgrade technical and management standards(organize skill development programs).

  • Expand coconut area under toddy tapping and give impetus to improve sap production industry.

  • Design and fabricate simple tools and equipment required by industry using appropriate technology.<
